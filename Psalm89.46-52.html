Psalm 89:46–52

John Gill's Unabridged Exposition with Parallel Spurgeon-Style Analytical Summaries
Verse 46
How long, Lord? wilt thou hide thyself, for ever?.... In the dark dispensations of providence, withdrawing his gracious presence, and withholding the influence of his love and grace; and this is repeated, and the expression of "for ever" added, to denote the deep sense the psalmist had of the distress, and the length of its duration, and the violent temptation he was under: shall thy wrath burn like fire? against thy people, as it seems to do in their apprehensions; and against thine anointed, the Messiah, when he bore the sins of his people, and suffered the penal effects of vindictive justice: for the wrath of God against sin is like fire, it cannot be quenched; it is intolerable, and melts the heart like wax; see Psalm 22:14.
  1. Lord, how long will You withdraw the light of Your gracious presence from us?
  2. The deep, protracted length of this dark trial breeds violent temptation in our souls.
  3. Your unquenchable wrath against sin burns like an intolerable, melting fire.
Verse 47
Remember how short my time is:.... The span of life is short, the end of it is near; it is but as a shadow, a vapour, a breath. The psalmist wishes the Lord to consider this, that he might have some joy and comfort before he died; or it may be a prayer of the Messiah, or of the church, representing him in his state of humiliation, praying that the time of his sufferings might be shortened, and his deliverance hastened: wherefore hast thou made all men in vain? no man is made in vain; every man is made for the glory of God, which is some way or other brought about by him: but the words may be rendered, "remember for what vanity thou hast made all the sons of men", or "brought them into being"; they being born to trouble, and their days few and full of evil; and their life, in its best estate, altogether vanity; which is the consequence of sin, and must be expected by all, till a new state of things takes place.
  1. Consider, O Lord, that human life is but a fleeting, passing shadow.
  2. Shorten our hours of suffering before we descend into the dark grave.
  3. No man is created without purpose, for all must ultimately glorify their Maker.
  4. Yet remember the present vanity and trouble into which fallen men are born.
Verse 48
What man is he that liveth, and shall not see death?.... Every man lives, or has a principle of life in him; but no man lives always, he must die: see death is to experience it; and this is common to all men, of every rank, sex, age, and character, high or low, rich or poor, good or bad; and there is no avoiding it, by wisdom, wealth, or power: shall he deliver his soul from the hand of the grave? Selah. Either from going down into it, or being detained in it; no man can do this; he cannot deliver his brother, nor can he give to God a ransom for himself, that he should live for ever, and not see corruption; see Psalm 49:7.
  1. Every living soul must eventually bow to the cold reality of death.
  2. No earthly rank, wealth, or human wisdom can ever exempt a man.
  3. No mortal possesses power to rescue his own soul from the grave's grasp.
Verse 49
Lord, where are thy former lovingkindnesses,.... The spiritual and eternal blessings of the covenant, which the psalmist called to mind, and which are of old, and are called the "sure mercies of David"; these are unchangeable and irreversible, though the church might seem to be deprived of them, or the comfort of them: which thou swarest unto David in thy truth? which were made to David, as a type of Christ, or to Christ himself, who is often called David; and which were confirmed by the oath of God, who is truth itself, and can never lie, nor suffer his faithfulness to fail.
  1. Where are those eternal, immutable covenant blessings promised of old to Your people?
  2. The church now laments the apparent loss of these sure mercies.
  3. Yet God swore these promises in truth to the true David, Jesus Christ.
Verse 50
Remember, Lord, the reproach of thy servants;.... Either the prophets of the Lord, or his saints, who were reproached by the Chaldeans, or by the enemies of Christ; who reproached his disciples and followers, and him himself; which is what is borne by him and his people, and is hard to bear; and therefore he prays that the Lord would remember it, and roll it away: how I do bear in my bosom the reproach of all the mighty people; or "many people": meaning the great men of the earth, the princes and potentates of it, who poured out their filth and sarcasm upon the psalmist, or upon the church of God; which was laid up in his heart, and sat heavy upon his spirit, and caused inward grief and sorrow; see Psalm 42:10.
  1. Look upon the bitter scoffs and reproaches aimed at Your chosen saints.
  2. The cruel taunts of the ungodly are exceedingly heavy for flesh to bear.
  3. The sharp, sarcastic arrows of the mighty pierce deep into our very hearts.
Verse 51
Wherewith thine enemies have reproached, O Lord;.... Which is an argument for pity and help; since they were the enemies of the Lord as well as of his people, and the reproach cast on them was cast on him: wherewith they have reproached the footsteps of thine anointed; or "Messiah": either his first coming, which the wicked Jews mocked at and scoffed at, because delayed, and they saw no appearance of it; or his second coming, which the scoffers of the last days would mock at; or the slow steps he took in bringing on the salvation of his people; or the path of afflictions and sufferings he trod in; or the holy walk and conversation of his members, who are the anointed of the Lord, which is reproached by the wicked.
  1. The mockers revile us because they hate the Lord Himself.
  2. They dare to scoff at the delayed steps of our Messiah's coming.
  3. The ungodly maliciously slander the afflicted paths and holy lives of His saints.
Verse 52
Blessed be the Lord for evermore,.... In this world, and in that to come, for all his mercies, and particularly for the covenant of grace, and the promises of it; which, notwithstanding all the objections made to them, and temptations to disbelief, are firm and sure: and this blessing of the Lord is an act of faith, viewing them in all dark dispensations, and expressing a full assurance of the completion of them; as well as is an antidote against the temptations, doubts, and fears, before suggested: Amen, and Amen; which is a double confirmation of what goes before; it is the seal of the covenant and of the promises of it, as well as of the faith of the church, and her desire to have it so. Here ends the third part of the book of Psalms.
  1. Bless the Lord eternally for His unchanging, glorious covenant of free grace.
  2. True faith praises God even while walking through the darkest providential dispensations.
  3. Our double 'Amen' seals this sure hope with triumphant, joyful certainty.
