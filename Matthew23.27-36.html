Matthew 23:27–36

Verse 27 Woe unto you Scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites. It is much these men could bear to hear themselves so often called by this name; and it shows great courage in our Lord, so freely to reprove them, and expose their wickedness, who were men of so much credit and influence with the people: Ifor ye are like unto whited sepulchres; or "covered with lime", as the Syriac, Arabic, and Persic versions, render it. For the Jews used to mark their graves with white lime, that they might be known: that so priests, Nazarites, and travellers, might avoid them, and not be polluted with them. This appears from various passages in their writings: IIwhich indeed appear beautiful outward; especially at a distance, and when new marked: IIIbut within are full of dead men's bones, and of all uncleanness; worms and rottenness, which arise from the putrefied carcasses, and are very nauseous and defiling. Of this marking of the graves, the reason of it, the time and manner of doing it, Maimonides gives us this account: "Whoever finds a grave, or a dead carcass, or anything for the dead that defiles, by the tent he is obliged to put a mark upon it, that it may not be a stumbling to others; and on the intermediate days of a feast, they go out from the sanhedrim, to mark the graves. With what do they mark? with chalk infused in water, and poured upon the unclean place: they do not put the mark upon the top of the unclean place, but so that the mark may stand out here and there, at the sides of the unclean place, that what is pure may not be corrupted; and they do not put the mark upon a place that is known, for it is known to all; but upon a place that is doubtful, as a field in which a grave is lost." Now because when the rains fell, these marks were washed away, hence on the first of Adar, when they used to repair the highways, they also marked the graves with white lime, that the graves might be seen and known, and avoided; and so on their intermediate feast days: the reason why they made use of chalk, or lime, was, because it looked white, and might be seen at a distance.
 
  1. Christ displays great courage by publically exposing the wickedness of the Scribes and Pharisees, who possess significant credit and influence with the people, comparing the Scribes and Pharisees to sepulchres covered with white lime.
  2. The Jews mark their graves with white lime so that the graves might be known, ensuring that priests, Nazarites, and travellers might avoid the graves and not be polluted by the graves, which marks cause the graves to appear beautiful outward, especially at a distance when newly marked.
  3. Though beautiful outward, the insides of the graves are full of dead men's bones, nauseous worms, and defiling rottenness that arise from putrefied carcasses.
Verse 28 IEven so ye also outwardly appear righteous unto men,.... By wearing long robes, broad phylacteries, and large fringes; by washing their cups and platters, and keeping them clean; by fastings, long prayers, and giving alms; and by paying tithes of all they possessed: IIbut within ye are full of hypocrisy and iniquity; which was covered over with these outward appearance of religion and holiness: they were full of hypocrisy, pretending to be what they were not; and full of "iniquity", or lawlessness; their hearts being packed with malice, envy, avarice, ambition, and all uncleanness.
 
  1. The Scribes and Pharisees outwardly appear righteous unto men through the actions of wearing long robes, broad phylacteries, large fringes, washing cups, fasting, making long prayers, giving alms, and paying tithes.
  2. The Scribes and Pharisees cover their inner state with an outward appearance of religion and holiness, though within they are full of hypocrisy, pretending to be what they are not, and full of lawlessness, with their hearts packed with malice, envy, avarice, ambition, and all uncleanness.
Verse 29 IWoe unto you Scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites, because ye build the tombs of the prophets,.... For it seems they did this, not from any real respect to the prophets, or any true value they had for their memories, or doctrine; but that they might be thought very holy and good men, and severe adjusters of the actions of their ancestors, who killed the prophets: and garnishing the sepulchres of the righteous; IIor orna-menting them, by adding to them new structures, or making beautiful additions to the old structures, or carving, gilding, or painting them, or casting white lime upon them.
 
  1. The Scribes and Pharisees build the tombs of the prophets, not from a real respect to the prophets or their doctrine, but so that the Scribes and Pharisees might be thought very holy and good men who severely adjust the actions of their ancestors.
  2. The Scribes and Pharisees ornament the sepulchres of the righteous by adding new structures, creating beautiful additions to the old structures, carving, gilding, painting, or casting white lime upon the sepulchres.
Verse 30 IAnd say, if we had been in the days of our fathers,.... Their ancestors and predecessors: signifying, that if they had lived in the times their ancestors lived, or had been in the same post and office with their ancestors, they should have opposed, at least not consented to the measures of their ancestors: IIwe would not have been partakers with them in the blood of the prophets; would not have joined their ancestors in persecuting the prophets, and in shedding the blood of the prophets, and putting the prophets to death; but would have received the prophets as the prophets of the Lord, have hearkened to the advice and message of the prophets, and have honoured and obeyed the prophets as such; and this the Scribes and Pharisees thought they sufficiently declared, by building and adorning the tombs of the prophets.
 
  1. The Scribes and Pharisees claim that if they had lived in the times of their ancestors, or occupied the same offices as their ancestors, they would have opposed or refused to consent to the measures of their ancestors.
  2. The Scribes and Pharisees claim they would not have joined their ancestors in persecuting the prophets or shedding the blood of the prophets, but would have received, hearkened to, and obeyed the prophets, believing that building and adorning the tombs of the prophets sufficiently declared this stance.
Verse 31 IWherefore ye be witnesses unto yourselves,.... By your own words and actions, by building and garnishing the tombs of the prophets, which is done under a show of respect to the prophets, but is a cover for your malice: IIthat ye are the children of them which killed the prophets; not only by natural descent, being lineally descended from them, which was their boast; but by a similitude of nature, temper, and disposition: they were the children of the murderers of the prophets, and were of the very same spirit, and were going to commit the same actions, and so were witnesses against themselves; they were witnesses that they were the children of murderers, and their actions quickly proved that they were the murderers themselves.
 
  1. The Scribes and Pharisees bear witness to themselves through their own words and actions, building and garnishing the tombs under a show of respect to the prophets, which acts serve as a cover for their malice.
  2. The Scribes and Pharisees witness that they are the children of those who killed the prophets, not merely by natural descent, but by a similitude of nature, temper, and disposition, proving they possess the same spirit and are going to commit the same actions as the original murderers.
Verse 32 IFill ye up then the measure of your fathers. Of their sins: for there is a certain measure of iniquity specified by God, for a nation, or a particular sort of people, like these Scribes and Pharisees, to fill up; which is known only to God: and when this measure is filled up, the judgments of God break forth upon them, to their utter ruin: IIthe ancestors of these men had gone a great way towards filling up the measure of national iniquity, by persecuting and murdering the prophets; and these men were quickly about to finish the measure, by crucifying the Son of God, and killing, scourging, and persecuting his apostles. The words are an ironical concession, or a prophecy of what they would do; and show that their destruction was inevitable, and that they were given up to a reprobate mind.
 
  1. God specifies a certain measure of iniquity for a nation or a particular group like the Scribes and Pharisees to fill up, which measure is known only to God, and when that measure is filled up, the judgments of God break forth to their utter ruin.
  2. The ancestors of the Scribes and Pharisees had gone a great way toward filling up the measure of national iniquity by persecuting the prophets, and these contemporary men are quickly about to finish the measure by crucifying the Son of God and persecuting his apostles.
Verse 33 IYe serpents, ye generation of vipers,.... As they were called by John the Baptist, Matthew 3:7. They were serpents for their subtlety, craft, and cunning; and vipers for their poisonous and venomous nature, their malice and wickedness: IIhow can ye escape the damnation of hell? or "the judgment of hell", as the Syriac version renders it; which is the true sense of the words: meaning, that they could not by any means escape that righteous judgment, and just punishment of their sins, which will be executed upon all wicked men in hell; and that it was a thing impossible that they should, continuing in their sins, in their hypocrisy and malice: nor could their formal outside religion, their rituals, and traditional washings, and tithes, secure them from the damnation of hell.
 
  1. The Scribes and Pharisees are called serpents for their subtlety, craft, and cunning, and they are called a generation of vipers for their poisonous nature, malice, and wickedness.
  2. It is a thing impossible for the Scribes and Pharisees to escape the righteous judgment and just punishment of hell while continuing in their sins, nor could their formal outside religion, rituals, traditional washings, and tithes secure them from the damnation of hell.
Verse 34 IWherefore behold, I send unto you prophets, and wise men, and scribes,.... Meaning his apostles, and the evangelists, and ministers of the word, who are called "prophets", because they had the gift of interpreting the Scriptures, and some of them of foretelling things to come; and "wise men", because they were filled with the wisdom of the Gospel, and were wise to win souls; and "scribes", because they were well instructed in the kingdom of heaven, and could bring out of their treasure things new and old: IIand some of them ye shall kill and crucify; as they did Stephen and James, whom they killed with the sword; and Peter, and others, whom they crucified; for this is a punishment they brought upon them by the hands of the Romans: and some of them shall ye scourge in your synagogues; as Peter and John, and the rest of the apostles, Acts 5:40, and Paul, 2 Corinthians 11:24: IIIand persecute them from city to city; as they did Paul and Barnabas, and others, from Antioch to Iconium, and from thence to Lystra and Derbe, and from place to place.
 
  1. Christ sends apostles, evangelists, and ministers of the word, who are called prophets because they interpret the Scriptures, wise men because they are filled with the wisdom of the Gospel, and scribes because they are well instructed in the kingdom of heaven.
  2. The Scribes and Pharisees shall kill, crucify, or scourge these ministers in their synagogues, bringing crucifixion upon the ministers by the hands of the Romans, as seen in the treatment of Stephen, James, Peter, and Paul.
  3. The Scribes and Pharisees shall persecute the ministers from city to city, driving the ministers from place to place as they did to Paul and Barnabas.
Verse 35 IThat upon you may come all the righteous blood,.... Or the blood of all the righteous men; shed upon the earth; from the blood of righteous Abel, unto the blood of Zacharias, son of Barachias, IIwhom ye slew between the temple and the altar: not that the Scribes and Pharisees personally slew these men, but because they were of the same principles, temper, and disposition with those who slew these men, and were going to commit the same or greater outrages; and by filling up the measure of national iniquity, would bring down the vengeance of God upon them, for all the righteous blood that had been shed from the beginning of the world: and because the blood of these two men is mentioned, it has given rise to various opinions who they were. IIIThe first, Abel, is well known, who was a righteous man, and was slain by his brother Cain; but who the last is, is not so clear: some think it is Zacharias the prophet, the son of Jehoiada, who was killed by the order of Joash, 2 Chronicles 24:20, and who, as he died, said, the Lord look upon it, and require it; and because he died with such a word in his mouth, Christ might rather mention his blood, as what would be required of this people: and he might be called the son of Barachias, either because his father had two names, Jehoiada and Barachias, which signify much the same thing, the praise or blessing of the Lord; or because he was his grandfather, and his father's name was Barachias.
 
  1. The vengeance of God will bring down upon the Scribes and Pharisees the punishment for all the blood of righteous men shed upon the earth, from the blood of righteous Abel unto the blood of Zacharias, son of Barachias.
  2. The Scribes and Pharisees are charged with the slaughter because they possess the same principles, temper, and disposition as those who actually slew these men, filling up the measure of national iniquity.
  3. The identity of the first victim, Abel, is well known as the righteous man slain by his brother Cain, but the identity of the last victim, Zacharias, is interpreted by some to be the prophet slain by the order of Joash, whose father Jehoiada may have also been known as Barachias.
Verse 36 Verily I say unto you,.... An usual form of introducing something of moment to raise attention to it, and to ascertain the truth of it: Iall these things shall come upon this generation; all the things which Christ had foretold should come to pass in the present age; as that the apostles and ministers of the word he should send to them, some of them they would kill and crucify, and others they would scourge in their synagogues, or persecute from place to place; and all the horrible murders and desolations, which should be the consequence of these things: IIand which came to pass, and had its full accomplishment about forty years after this, in the utter destruction of Jerusalem, and the whole nation; so that many now living were personally involved in that temporal ruin, as well as escaped not the damnation of hell.
 
  1. Christ uses an usual form of introducing a momentous matter to ascertain its truth, declaring that all the foretold crimes against the sent apostles and ministers will come upon the present age.
  2. The consequences, consisting of horrible murders and desolations, had their full accomplishment about forty years after this prophecy in the utter destruction of Jerusalem and the whole nation, personally involving many then living in temporal ruin and the damnation of hell.
