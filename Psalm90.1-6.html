Psalm 90:1–6

John Gill's Exposition of the Entire Bible with Concise Summaries

Verse 1

Lord, thou hast been our dwelling place in all generations. IWhose names are written in the Lamb's book of life; who have a being in Christ, and are preserved in him; and who believe in him, and pass from death to life: these are "the generation of the righteous," the spiritual seed of Christ. IINow the Lord, the "Adonai", the Word and Son of God, is the dwelling place of these; they dwelt in his heart from everlasting, being loved by him with an everlasting love; they dwelt in him as their covenant head and representative, before the world began; and they dwell in him as their refuge, shelter, and strong tower, in time, and shall dwell with him to all eternity. IIISo the Targum, "Lord, the glory of thy Shechinah hast thou made a habitation for us in every generation." And so it has been in every age, from the beginning of the world; the saints have always found a safe and comfortable dwelling place in Christ, who is the same yesterday, to day, and for ever.

  1. Election and union with Christ define the specific generation of the righteous.
  2. The divine Word serves as the eternal covenant head, refuge, and ultimate shelter for believers.
  3. Saints across every historic epoch consistently find immutable security and spiritual habitation in Christ.
Verse 2

Before the mountains were brought forth, IWhich are the most ancient parts of the earth, coeval with it, and are the most solid and durable parts of it, stable and immovable; or ever thou hadst formed the earth and the world: IIthe whole terraqueous globe, the land and the sea, and the habitable parts of it, with all the plots and positions of it: even from everlasting to everlasting, thou art God; IIIand so are his love, his covenant, his choices, and his promises; and this is a great ground of comfort to his people, who are his dwelling place: he was God before the world was, and will be so when it is no more; and he is the same from eternity to eternity, in his nature, perfections, and purposes; and Christ, who is this Adonai or Lord, is eternal; he was set up from everlasting as Mediator; his goings forth were of old, from everlasting; he is the alpha and omega, the first and the last.

  1. The eternal nature of God predates the formation of ancient, stable physical mountains.
  2. Divine existence precedes the creation of the entire terraqueous, habitable globe.
  3. God remains completely immutable and eternal in nature, attributes, covenant promises, and mediatorial office.
Verse 3

Thou turnest man to destruction, IOr to the dust; to death, which is the destruction of man; not an annihilation of body or soul, but a dissolution of the union between them: and this is the effect of sin, and the sentence of the law for it; and this is passed on all men by the Lord himself, who brings them to the grave, and reduces their bodies to dust: and sayest, Return, ye children of men; IIfrom whence ye came; or return to dust, as the Targum; see (Gen. 3:19). IIISome understand this of the resurrection of the dead, which will be by the word and voice of Christ, calling them to return to life, to return to their bodies again; but the context seems to require the former sense, which is the death of men, and their return to the earth, which is daily done by the order and appointment of God.

  1. Death constitutes a dissolution of body and soul, reducing physical frames to dust as a consequence of sin.
  2. The sovereign decree commands all human beings to return to their original earthly elements.
  3. While alternative interpretations suggest a resurrection call, the immediate context emphasizes mortal dissolution.
Verse 4

For a thousand years in thy sight are but as yesterday when it is past, IWhich is as nothing, and can no more be recalled; and though a thousand years is a long time to man, it is as nothing to God, who is eternal; he is not affected by the length of time, nor does he measure things by it: and as a watch in the night; IIwhich was but three or four hours long, and which passed away while men were asleep, and was as nothing to them: IIIthe phrase "in thy sight" is a clear proof that this is to be understood of God's eternity, and of the disproportion of time to it; and not of the life of man, or of the thousands of years the world should continue, as some have thought; but it shows that the long lives of the antediluvians were as nothing to God, and much less the short lives of men now.

  1. A thousand years is entirely negligible to an eternal God who exists outside temporal measurements.
  2. Vast temporal durations correspond to a brief nightly watch, passing unnoticed by those asleep.
  3. The specific phrasing establishes absolute divine eternity rather than human lifespans or millennial frameworks.
Verse 5

Thou carriest them away as with a flood, IBy death, which is compared to a flood, for its force and power, which nothing can withstand; and for its sweeping all before it, without respect to persons; and for its suddenness, and the swiftness of its motion: they are as a sleep; IItheir life is a sleep, a state of insensibility, inactivity, and security, till death comes; and death itself is a sleep, a long sleep, from which there is no awaking till the resurrection morning: in the morning they are like grass which groweth up; IIIwhich looks green and beautiful, and promises a long duration; so man in his youth, and health, and prosperity, looks gay and pleasant, and thinks to live long; but it is a short-lived thing, soon cut down, as follows.

  1. Death acts like an unstoppable, sudden, and non-respecter flood sweeping away all human life.
  2. Mortal existence and death are characterized by an unconscious, inactive state akin to sleep.
  3. Human youth and temporary prosperity resemble green grass, blooming beautifully but possessing short-lived durability.
Verse 6

In the morning it flourisheth, and groweth up; Ibeing watered with the dew, and cheered with the beams of the sun; so man in his infancy and youth grows up in the stature of his body, and the faculties of his mind, and flourishes in the health and vigor of his animal spirits: in the evening it is cut down, and withereth; IIby the scythe of the mower; so man in old age, or by diseases, is cut down by the scythe of death, and his body becomes lifeless and breathless, and is laid in the grave, and rot there: IIIthis is an elegant description of the frailty and mortality of man, which is often used in the sacred Scriptures; see (Job 14:1,2) (Ps. 102:11) (Isa. 40:6-8) (1 Pet. 1:24).

  1. Morning growth represents the early development, physical stature, and vigor of human youth.
  2. Evening cutting reflects old age or disease terminating life via the sudden scythe of death.
  3. The scriptural metaphor elegantly illustrates systemic human frailty, transience, and certain physical mortality.
