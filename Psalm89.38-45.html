An Exposition of Psalm 89:38-45

By John Gill, D.D. (1697–1771) — Structural Digest Edition

Psalm 89:38
But thou hast cast off and abhorred, thou hast been wroth with thine anointed. This and the following verses are the expansion of the complaint in Psalm 89:38-45, and show the state of the matter to be quite different from what is before promised; the promises were made to David, but the reverse of them happened to his posterity, which is the ground of the expostulation; and this is to be understood either of the afflictions of the people of Israel under the Babylonish captivity, or under the persecutions of Antiochus, or of the distresses of the church of Christ under Rome Pagan or Papal, or of the sufferings of Christ himself, when he was forsaken by his Father, and bore his wrath; or of the cases of private believers, who, through desertion, or the pressure of affliction, are ready to think that God has cast them off, and is wroth with them, and abhors them: but the words are chiefly to be understood of the kingly power, or of the kingdom of David, which seemed to be cast off and abhorred when it was destroyed, and the king carried captive; and this was done in the wrath of God, which he broke forth upon them for their sins.
  1. Expounds a dramatic divergence between prior messianic covenant promises and the historic, dismal reality of David's royal line.
  2. Applies the text broadly to the Babylonian captivity, Maccabean persecutions, and the generic trials of the corporate church.
  3. Relates the text spiritually to the absolute penal isolation and divine wrath borne substitutionally by Christ on the cross.
  4. Focuses principally on the historic dismantling and captivity of the literal, earthly Davidic monarchy under divine judgment.
Psalm 89:39
Thou hast made void the covenant of thy servant,.... Or "abhorred" it, as the word is by some translated; it seemed to be broken, and its promises fail, and the blessings of it withheld; though it was not really made void, for God's covenant cannot be broken, it is sure and unalterable: thou hast profaned his crown, by casting it to the ground; by suffering the king to be taken and bound, and his crown taken from his head, and his kingly dignity trampled upon; as it was by Nebuchadnezzar, when he took Zedekiah, bound him in chains, and carried him to Babylon, and put out his eyes; and as it was by the Romans, when they took away the place and nation of the Jews, and their kingly power; and as it was by the persecuting Emperors, when they cast down the Christian empire; and as it is by Antichrist, who tramples upon the kingly authority of Christ, and the laws of his house.
  1. Defends covenantal immutability by clarifying that the covenant only "seemed" voided, broken, or frustrated to human sight.
  2. Defines the profanation of the crown via the historical, physical dethronement and horrific blinding of King Zedekiah.
  3. Traces this defilement through the Roman devastation of Jerusalem and the imperial pagan persecutions of early Christians.
  4. Exposes Antichrist's contemporary blasphemous subversion of Christ’s supreme kingly authority and sovereign ecclesiastical laws.
Psalm 89:40
Thou hast broken down all his hedges,.... All his walls and fortifications, all the places of defense about Jerusalem, and other cities in Judea; which were the protection of the king and his subjects, and might be said to be the Lord's, because he was the protector of them, or because he suffered them to be broken down, or did it himself by the hands of the Chaldeans or Romans: thou hast brought his strong holds to ruin; his fortified cities, and the fortresses in them, particularly the city of Jerusalem, which was well fortified; these were demolished and ruined, and became a heap of stones.
  1. Identifies the metaphorical broken hedges as the literal stone walls and military fortifications protecting Judea.
  2. Attributes the active destruction ultimately to God's sovereign decree executed through Chaldean and Roman instruments.
  3. Maintains that Jerusalem's strongest military keeps were thoroughly pulverized into completely uninhabitable heaps of rubble.
Psalm 89:41
All that pass by the way spoil him,.... Strip him of his wealth and substance, his crowns and sceptre, his jewels and precious things; as did the Chaldeans, and afterwards the Romans; and as did the thieves that passed by when Christ was crucified, who parted his garments among them: he is a reproach to his neighbours; they scoffed at the king, and at his people, and mocked at their miseries; as did the Edomites, and others, at the time of the Babylonish captivity; and as did the scribes and Pharisees at Christ, when he was on the cross; and as the antichristian party do at the true church and followers of Christ.
  1. Illustrates the physical plundering of the Davidic dynasty's royal wealth and crown treasures by foreign armies.
  2. Typologically connects this specific spoiling to the Roman soldiers casting lots for Christ's garments at His crucifixion.
  3. Notes the vicious geopolitical mockery leveled against the conquered Jews by neighboring nations like the Edomites.
  4. Parallels this historic shame to the pharisaical mocking of Christ and Antichrist's abuse of the true church.
Psalm 89:42
Thou hast set up the right hand of his adversaries,.... Given them power and strength, and made them superior to him, so that they have prevailed over him, and gained a conquest: this was done when the Lord left the king and his army, and went not forth with them, but suffered them to fall into the hands of the enemy; which was owing to his sovereign will and pleasure, and the kindle of his wrath against them for their sins: thou hast made all his enemies to rejoice; at his destruction, and the miseries he was brought into; as they did, when he was carried captive, and his city destroyed; and as the enemies of Christ did, when he was crucified; and as the followers of antichrist will, when the witnesses are slain.
  1. Attributes the military dominance of the adversaries to God's sovereign empowerment rather than their own inherent might.
  2. Traces this defeat to God justly abandoning the apostate army to the fury of his holy wrath.
  3. Examines the malicious celebration of the enemies over the historical captivity and architectural ruin of Jerusalem.
  4. Connects this wicked joy to the execution of Christ and the future killing of the two apocalyptic witnesses.
Psalm 89:43
Thou hast also turned the edge of his sword,.... Blunted it, so that it could not do execution; or turned it back, so that it was so far from hurting the enemy, that it turned upon himself; or shifted it, that he could not keep his ground, but was obliged to fly; all which are sometimes the cases of armies: and hast not made him to stand in the battle; but to give way, to turn his back, and flee; no courage being given him, nor success; but panic fears seized him, and he fell before his enemy.
  1. Interprets the blunted sword as a total loss of offensive military execution and defensive martial efficacy.
  2. Suggests that the weapon was terrifyingly turned inward upon the army itself, causing widespread battlefield panic.
  3. Notes that God completely stripped the soldiers of natural courage, forcing an ignominious retreat before the enemy.
Psalm 89:44
Thou hast made his glory to cease,.... His royal glory, his kingly dignity, the splendor of his kingdom, which ceased when it was destroyed, and he carried captive; and the glory of the priesthood, which ceased when the temple was burnt; and the glory of the nation, when they were no more a free people, but servants to a foreign power: and cast his throne down to the ground; meaning the same thing, the destruction of the kingdom, and the cessation of the kingly power; the throne of David, which was promised to be established for ever, now seeming to be entirely ruined and demised.
  1. Deals with the systemic eradication of the civil, royal glory and political splendor of the Jewish kingdom.
  2. Chronicles the absolute end of the Levitical priesthood resulting directly from the burning of the temple.
  3. Addresses the agonizing theological tension of David's seemingly ruined eternal throne lying shattered on the ground.
Psalm 89:45
The days of his youth hast thou shortened,.... The days of his kingdom, or of his kingly power; which did not flourish and continue long, but was soon cut off: the kingdom of Judah, from the time of David to the captivity, was but upwards of four hundred years; and the reign of some of its kings was very short, especially the last, who were in the prime of their days, as Jehoahaz, Jehoiakim, Jehoiachin, and Zedekiah; and so the kingdom itself was cut off in its youth, before it came to that age and glory it might have arrived unto: thou hast covered him with shame. Selah. By taking away his crown and kingdom, making him a captive, and the mock of his enemies; and this was true of Christ, when he was covered with shame and spitting in the hall of the high priest, and when he was mocked by the soldiers on the cross; and of his people, when they are reproached for his sake.
  1. Interprets the "shortened youth" as the premature geopolitical demise of the Davidic dynasty after only four centuries.
  2. Highlights the final, youthful monarchs whose brief reigns ended abruptly in chains and humiliation.
  3. Explicates the severe, crushing shame of the king being made a public mockery to hostile pagan empires.
  4. Brings the text to a christological climax by mapping this shame to Christ's mocking and spitting during crucifixion.
