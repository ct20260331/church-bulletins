Matthew 22:34–45

John Gill's Exposition of the Entire New Testament — Covenantal & Exegetical Synopses

Verse 34

But when the Pharisees had heard that he had put the Sadducees to silence,.... By a most clear, solid, and convincing argument, fetched out of the writings of Moses, which they themselves admitted: they were gathered together; not to congratulate him on his victory, or to thank him for defending the doctrine of the resurrection, which they also held; but either to plot some new mischief against him, or to find out some word or action of his that they could criticize, hoping to regain the credit they had lost among the people.

Synopsis

  1. Christ decisively silences the Sadducees using Mosaic proofs.
  2. Pharisees assemble not out of gratitude, but political malice.
  3. The gathering aims to restore lost rabbinical authority and prestige.
  4. They seek fresh pretexts to entrap Christ before the multitudes.

Verse 35

Then one of them, which was a lawyer,.... A scribe, as he is called in Mark 12:28 one whose business it was to read, interpret, and expound the law of Moses to the people. He was put forward by the rest as the most subtle and learned among them, to try his skill on Jesus. He asked him a question, tempting him, and saying; trying not so much to learn anything from him, but to see if he could draw him into an answer that contradicted the received traditions of the elders or Moses.

Synopsis

  1. The interrogator is identified as a professional scribe or lawyer.
  2. He is selected as the vanguard due to superior academic cunning.
  3. The motive is overtly adversarial rather than pedagogical.
  4. The query attempts to force a contradiction with Mosaic tradition.

Verse 36

Master, which is the great commandment in the law?.... This question was proposed with an insidious intent, because the Jews categorized the commandments into "great" and "small," "weighty" and "light." They often debated which was the most foundational, some favoring circumcision, others the sabbath, others sacrifices. Whichever way Christ answered, they hoped to accuse him of deprecating the remaining laws or violating established rabbinic consensus.

Synopsis

  1. The title "Master" is used insincerely to mask hostile intent.
  2. Reflects contemporary Jewish divisions over law classification.
  3. Rabbis debated priority among circumcision, sabbath, and sacrifice.
  4. Designed to isolate Christ from competing theological factions.

Verse 37

Jesus said unto him, Thou shalt love the Lord thy God.... Christ answers by citing the Shema from Deuteronomy 6:5. This requires supreme, absolute affection toward God as the covenant Lord. It commands the exercise of the whole heart, soul, and mind—meaning all powers of the human soul must be intensely directed toward God. This cuts through human traditions to demand total internal spiritual devotion rather than mere externalism.

Synopsis

  1. Christ appeals directly to Deuteronomy to define supreme duty.
  2. Demands absolute devotion to God as the Covenant Jehovah.
  3. Enumerates the total commitment of all human faculties.
  4. Exposes and condemns the superficiality of mere pharisaic externalism.

Verse 38

This is the first and great commandment..... It is the first in order, dignity, and nature, flowing directly from our relation to God as Creator and Sovereign. It is the greatest in its object, which is God Himself, and in its scope, as it undergirds and animates obedience to all other individual precepts. Without this love, any outward compliance with other laws is dead, hypocritical, and empty before God.

Synopsis

  1. Establishes priority based on natural and covenantal order.
  2. Designated greatest because its direct object is God Himself.
  3. Serves as the internal, animating principle of true obedience.
  4. Declares outward duty worthless if detached from divine love.

Verse 39

And the second is like unto it,.... Equal in authority, derived from the same divine Lawgiver, and written upon the same tables of stone. Thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself. This sums up the second table of the Decalogue. It demands that we desire and promote the spiritual and temporal well-being of our fellow men with the same sincerity and constancy that we naturally apply toward our own preservation and comfort.

Synopsis

  1. The second table shares the same divine, authoritative origin.
  2. Summarizes all horizontal, human-ward moral obligations.
  3. Establishes self-love as the measure for neighborly benevolence.
  4. Requires active promotion of another's temporal and spiritual good.

Verse 40

On these two commandments hang all the law and the prophets..... All the moral precepts found in the five books of Moses and the subsequent writings of the prophets are merely developments, expansions, or applications of these two fundamental principles. They contain the sum and substance of all practical religion. Whatever is commanded or forbidden in Scripture can be traced back directly to love for God or love for man.

Synopsis

  1. Asserts that all Old Testament revelation depends on these concepts.
  2. Reduces the entire moral code to two foundational pillars.
  3. Frames all prophetic warnings as enforcement of these principles.
  4. Anchors all biblical ethics in unified love for God and man.

Verse 41

While the Pharisees were gathered together,.... Still remaining in a body after their lawyer had questioned Jesus, and while they were rejoicing in his attempt or waiting for a chance to intercept. Jesus asked them; turning the tables on them, not to evade their malice, but to demonstrate their profound ignorance of the very Scriptures they claimed to interpret, specifically concerning the person of the Messiah.

Synopsis

  1. The Pharisees remain assembled to maintain corporate opposition.
  2. Christ initiates a proactive cross-examination of his opponents.
  3. The counter-query intends to expose deep pharisaic blindness.
  4. Shifts the debate from ethics to Christological ontology.

Verse 42

Saying, What think ye of Christ? whose son is he?.... He asks what their theological view is regarding the lineage and nature of the promised Messiah. They say unto him, The son of David. This was the standard, correct, yet superficial answer maintained by the Jewish schools. They expected a merely human king who would inherit David's literal throne and liberate them from Roman bondage, overlooking his divine nature.

Synopsis

  1. Demands an evaluation of the Messiah's parentage and nature.
  2. Pharisees provide the conventional, accurate, yet limited response.
  3. They confess the Davidic lineage according to prophetic texts.
  4. Their answer betrays an expectation of a merely human deliverer.

Verse 43

He saith unto them, How then doth David in spirit call him Lord?.... If he is merely a human descendant, how can David, writing under the direct inspiration of the Holy Spirit in Psalm 110:1, address his own son by a title of sovereign lordship? A father never calls his son "Lord" in the patriarchal framework of Israel. Christ shows that their purely natural, earthly Christology cannot reconcile David's inspired language.

Synopsis

  1. Appeals directly to Psalm 110 as inspired scripture.
  2. Highlights a major anomaly within patriarchal family structures.
  3. Challenges a naturalistic, non-divine view of the Messiah.
  4. Exposes the insufficiency of their flat hermeneutic model.

Verse 44

The Lord said unto my Lord, Sit thou on my right hand,.... Citing the specific covenantal declaration where Jehovah addresses the Messiah. It displays the Father exalting the Son to equal majesty, power, and universal dominion following his work on earth. Till I make thine enemies thy footstool; guaranteeing the absolute, progressive subjugation of all opposing powers, demonic and human, under Christ’s sovereign rule.

Synopsis

  1. Cites the inter-trinitarian decree between Father and Son.
  2. Exhibits the post-resurrection session of Christ at the right hand.
  3. Declares the total, supreme dominion granted to the Messiah.
  4. Promises the ultimate defeat of all cosmic and earthly enemies.

Verse 45

If David then call him Lord, how is he his son?.... This final, devastating question seals their lips. The dilemma can only be resolved by acknowledging the dual nature of the Messiah: He is David’s Lord according to his divine nature, being the eternal Son of God, and David’s son according to the flesh, born of his lineage. By failing to see his divinity, the Pharisees prove they do not know the true Messiah.

Synopsis

  1. Presents the definitive, unanswerable theological dilemma.
  2. Can only be solved by acknowledging the Hypostatic Union.
  3. Affirms Messiah as both eternal Lord and human descendant.
  4. Exposes the complete failure of the pharisaic system.
