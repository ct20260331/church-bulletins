Exposition of the New Testament

Matthew 21:33-46 — The Parable of the Wicked Husbandmen by John Gill (1697–1771)
Matthew 21:33

Hear another parable: There is a certain householder, which planted a vineyard, and hedged it round about, and digged a winepress in it, and built a tower, and let it out to husbandmen, and went into a far country. This parable represents the state and condition of the Jewish church. The householder is God the Father, who chosen the people of Israel to be a peculiar people to himself. The vineyard is the house of Israel and the men of Judah, planted with the choicest vine, set apart for his service and glory. The hedging it round about signifies the divine protection and the special laws, such as the ceremonial and civil institutes, which distinguished them from other nations. The winepress digged in it refers to the altar of burnt offerings, or the temple itself, where the blood of sacrifices was poured out like wine. The tower built in it is the temple or the city of Jerusalem, built for defense and for the watchmen, the priests and prophets, to look out from. Letting it out to husbandmen means committing the care of the people's souls and the instruction of them to the ecclesiastical rulers, such as the priests, Levites, Scribes, and Pharisees. Going into a far country signifies God's withdrawing his immediate visible presence, leaving them to the ordinary means of grace and their own stewardship for a long season.

  1. Identifies the householder as God the Father and the vineyard as the chosen nation of Israel.
  2. Explains the hedge, winepress, and tower as divine protection, the altar/temple, and Jerusalem's spiritual watchmen.
  3. Defines the husbandmen as the Jewish ecclesiastical leaders entrusted with spiritual care.
  4. Interprets the householder's departure as God leaving the nation to their own stewardship for a long season.
Matthew 21:34

And when the time of the fruit drew near, he sent his servants to the husbandmen, that they might receive the fruits of it. The time of fruit means the season when God might reasonably expect fruits of righteousness, obedience, and repentance from the Jewish nation, in proportion to the advantages they enjoyed. The servants sent are the prophets of the Old Testament, such as Elijah, Elisha, Isaiah, and Jeremiah. They were sent to the husbandmen, the rulers of the Jewish church, to demand the fruits of true religion and legal obedience due to God. This shows that God expects regular returns of gratitude, praise, and holiness from those who enjoy special privileges under his care and cultivation.

  1. Defines the fruit season as the period when God expected righteous obedience from Israel.
  2. Identifies the sent servants as Old Testament prophets sent to demand true religion from the rulers.
  3. Affirms that God expects regular returns of holiness from those granted special privileges.
Matthew 21:35

And the husbandmen took his servants, and beat one, and killed another, and stoned another. This describes the cruel and injurious treatment the prophets met with from the Jewish rulers. They beat one, as they did Jeremiah and Micaiah; they killed another with the sword, as they did Zechariah the son of Jehoiada, and many others in the time of Jezebel; they stoned another, as they did Zechariah in the court of the Lord's house. This portrays the extreme obstinacy, rebellion, and malice of the Jewish priests and rulers against God, his messages, and his messengers, abusing those who came to reform them.

  1. Details the violent treatment of the prophets, citing historical instances of beating and execution.
  2. Maps specific biblical figures like Jeremiah and Zechariah to the respective modes of martyrdom.
  3. Exposes the extreme rebellion and deep-seated malice of the Jewish leadership against divine reformers.
Matthew 21:36

Again, he sent other servants more than the first: and they did unto them likewise. The other servants mean a later succession of prophets, or a larger number of them, who were sent in subsequent reigns to reclaim the people. God's patience and forbearance are shown in sending message after message despite previous ill-treatment. The husbandmen did to them likewise, showing that their malice was habitual and progressive, as they continued to persecute, reject, and murder successive messengers without remorse, filling up the measure of their fathers' iniquity.

  1. Interprets the second group of servants as a later, larger succession of Old Testament prophets.
  2. Highlights God's enduring patience and long-suffering mercy in sending repeated warnings despite prior violence.
  3. Notes the progressive, habitual nature of the leaders' malice as they continued their persecution without remorse.
Matthew 21:37

But last of all he sent unto them his son, saying, They will reverence my son. This refers to the mission of Jesus Christ, the only begotten Son of God, sent in the fullness of time. He was sent 'last of all' because no more prophets or messengers were to be sent after him to that nation. The phrase 'they will reverence my son' does not imply that God was ignorant of the event, but expresses what might reasonably be expected from human beings towards one of such dignity, authority, and excellence. It highlights the high degree of expectation and the weight of responsibility placed upon the Jewish rulers to receive the Messiah.

  1. Identifies the final messenger as Jesus Christ, the unique Son of God sent in the fullness of time.
  2. Clarifies that 'they will reverence my son' denotes reasonable human expectation rather than divine ignorance.
  3. Emphasizes the ultimate weight of responsibility placed upon the Jewish rulers to accept the Messiah.
Matthew 21:38

But when the husbandmen saw the son, they said among themselves, This is the heir; come, let us kill him, and let us seize on his inheritance. The husbandmen saw the son through his miracles, doctrine, and holy life, which demonstrated his identity. They said among themselves, acknowledging in their councils that he was indeed the promised Messiah or a great threat to them. Calling him 'the heir' points to Christ's rightful lordship over the Jewish church and all things as the Son of God. Their conspiracy to 'kill him' signifies the formal resolution of the Sanhedrin to take away his life. To 'seize on his inheritance' means their desire to maintain their own worldly power, authority, and prestige over the people, which they feared losing if Christ was acknowledged.

  1. Explains that the rulers recognized Christ's identity through his undeniable miracles, teaching, and life.
  2. Defines the term 'heir' as Christ's rightful lordship over the church and all creation.
  3. Exposes their conspiracy to kill him as a deliberate effort to preserve their own worldly authority.
Matthew 21:39

And they caught him, and cast him out of the vineyard, and slew him. Catching him denotes the apprehension of Christ in the garden by Judas and the officers. Casting him out of the vineyard signifies delivering him over to the Gentiles, and his suffering outside the gates of Jerusalem, showing his rejection by the nation. Slaying him refers to his actual crucifixion and death on the cross at Calvary. This acts as a literal prophecy of Christ's upcoming passion and death at the hands of the chief priests.

  1. Traces 'catching him' to Christ's physical betrayal and arrest in the Garden of Gethsemane.
  2. Interprets 'casting him out' as delivering Christ to the Gentiles to suffer outside Jerusalem's gates.
  3. Views the entire verse as a direct, literal prophecy of Christ's upcoming crucifixion and passion.
Matthew 21:40

When the lord therefore of the vineyard cometh, what will he do unto those husbandmen? This question is put to the Scribes and Pharisees to make them judges of their own case and bring conviction. The coming of the lord of the vineyard does not mean the final judgment, but the execution of his vengeance upon the Jewish nation by the Roman armies. It appeals to their sense of common justice to determine what punishment is appropriate for such unparalleled wickedness and rebellion.

  1. Explains that the question forces the Scribes and Pharisees to pass judgment on their own conduct.
  2. Identifies the lord's coming not as final judgment, but as temporal wrath via Roman armies.
  3. Appeals to fundamental standards of justice to highlight the severity of the husbandmen's rebellion.
Matthew 21:41

They say unto him, He will miserably destroy those wicked men, and will let out his vineyard unto other husbandmen, which shall render him the fruits in their seasons. The Jewish rulers unwittingly pass a righteous sentence upon themselves, acknowledging the justice of their ruin. Miserably destroying those wicked men was fulfilled in the terrible destruction of Jerusalem and the slaughter of the Jews. Letting out the vineyard to other husbandmen signifies the transfer of church privileges and the Gospel dispensation to the Gentiles. These new husbandmen, the Gentile pastors and churches, would render fruit by preaching and living according to the truths of God's Word in their seasons.

  1. Notes that the rulers blindly pronounce a completely accurate sentence of condemnation upon themselves.
  2. Connects the miserable destruction to the historical sack of Jerusalem and slaughter of its people.
  3. Interprets the new husbandmen as Gentile pastors and churches who would yield faithful spiritual fruit.
Matthew 21:42

Jesus saith unto them, Did ye never read in the scriptures, The stone which the builders rejected, the same is become the head of the corner: this is the Lord's doing, and it is marvellous in our eyes? Christ quotes Psalm 118 to show that their rejection of him was predicted by scripture. The stone represents Christ himself, durable, strong, and a sure foundation. The builders are the Jewish rulers, priests, and Scribes, who were supposed to build up the church but rejected him. Becoming the head of the corner signifies Christ's exaltation to the highest place of honor, uniting Jews and Gentiles into one spiritual building. This transformation is entirely the Lord's doing, displaying divine power, and is marvelous to all true believers who behold his grace.

  1. Analyzes the architectural metaphor, identifying Christ as the strong, durable foundation stone.
  2. Defines the builders as the religious leaders who actively rejected him instead of building the church.
  3. Exalts Christ's corner-stone position as a divine marvel that unifies Jewish and Gentile believers.
Matthew 21:43

Therefore say I unto you, The kingdom of God shall be taken from you, and given to a nation bringing forth the fruits thereof. The kingdom of God here signifies the external administration of the Gospel, its ordinances, and privileges. Taking it away from them means the removal of the visible church state from the Jewish nation for their unbelief. Giving it to a nation refers to the Gentile world, which is chosen to form a holy nation and a peculiar people. Bringing forth the fruits thereof implies that the Gentiles would receive the Gospel with faith, producing love, obedience, and good works, which the Jews failed to perform.

  1. Defines the 'kingdom of God' as the gospel's outward administration, ordinances, and privileges.
  2. Explains that taking the kingdom away represents the dissolution of the visible Jewish church state.
  3. Identifies the recipient nation as the Gentile world, destined to produce active faith and obedience.
Matthew 21:44

And whosoever shall fall on this stone shall be broken: but on whomsoever it shall fall, it will grind him to powder. Falling on this stone means stumbling at Christ through unbelief and taking offense at his low estate during his life. Being broken signifies the spiritual damage, guilt, and ruin such individuals bring upon themselves, though recoverable. But on whomsoever it shall fall refers to Christ coming in judgment against the obstinate and impenitent. Grinding him to powder denotes total, irrecoverable destruction and ruin, which fell upon the Jewish nation at Jerusalem's destruction and will fall on all Christ's enemies at the last day.

  1. Distinguishes between stumbling out of unbelief during Christ's life and facing his final judgment.
  2. Defines the resulting brokenness as serious spiritual damage, which remains individually recoverable.
  3. Warns that being ground to powder signifies total, irrecoverable destruction for all persistent enemies.
Matthew 21:45

And when the chief priests and Pharisees had heard his parables, they perceived that he spake of them. The chief priests and Pharisees heard these parables and clearly understood their application. They perceived that they were the wicked husbandmen who abused the servants and killed the son. This conviction did not lead to repentance, but rather inflamed their resentment and deepened their guilt, as they saw their own characters accurately exposed and their destruction openly threatened.

  1. Confirms that the chief priests and Pharisees fully understood that the parable targeted them directly.
  2. Observes that this internal conviction failed to produce genuine repentance or self-reflection.
  3. Shows that the truth instead inflamed their resentment, deepening their guilt and hostility.
Matthew 21:46

But when they sought to lay hands on him, they feared the multitude, because they took him for a prophet. Seeking to lay hands on him shows their immediate, malicious desire to apprehend Christ and put him to death. They feared the multitude, which acted as a divine restraint upon them, preventing them from executing their design. The multitude took him for a prophet, holding Christ in high esteem because of his doctrine and miracles. This popular regard forced the rulers to delay their plots until a more favorable and private opportunity arose.

  1. Highlights the immediate, malicious impulse of the leaders to seize Christ and execute him.
  2. Attributes their restraint to a fear of the public, which acted as a divine check on their designs.
  3. Notes that the multitude's high view of Christ as a prophet forced the conspiracy into temporary delay.
