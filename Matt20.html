QUESTION 1 · Matthew 20:20–22
What was wrong with the request that the mother of James and John made?
🔹 SOCRATIC HINT — Read vv. 17–19, then vv. 20–21

Jesus has just finished telling His disciples He is about to be mocked, flogged, and crucified. Then immediately the mother of James and John asks for throne seats. What does that timing reveal about what kind of kingdom they were still imagining?

→ They were still imagining an earthly kingdom of power and honour — they had completely missed that the path to Christ's glory runs through the cross.

🔹 SOCRATIC HINT

Verse 23 says the seats of honour "belong to those for whom they have been prepared by My Father." If God's eternal decree already determines who stands nearest to Christ in glory, what does a prayer trying to re-negotiate that reveal about the one praying?

→ It reveals ignorance of both God's sovereignty and the true nature of the kingdom — we cannot lobby God to override what He has eternally decreed.

📖 After Q1 — ~330 words · ~2:32 elapsed
FULL ANSWER (Reference — hidden, does not affect timing)

The request had two serious errors. First, it showed a completely carnal, earthly understanding of Christ's kingdom — as if the highest positions in it were thrones of worldly honour to be secured by a well-placed petition from the right person (their mother). Second, it presumed that the Father's secret decrees about who stands nearest to Christ in glory could be altered by a human request. Both errors come from the same root: a failure to understand that the kingdom of heaven is not run on the same principles as the kingdoms of this world.

Christ's response redirects their ambition entirely: He does not say "bad timing, ask again later." He asks whether they are prepared to drink the cup He is about to drink — the cup of suffering — because the path to glory in His kingdom runs through the cross, not around it.

QUESTION 2 · Matthew 20:22–23
What is the "cup" Jesus speaks of — and did James and John understand what they were agreeing to?
🔹 SOCRATIC HINT — Search the Old Testament

Read Psalm 75:8 and Isaiah 51:17 below. In those passages, what does God's "cup" represent — is it something pleasant, or something terrifying?

→ It is the cup of God's wrath against sin — something terrifying, not merely a symbol of gentle hardship.

📖 CROSS-REFERENCES
Psalm 75:8 (BSB) "For in the LORD's hand is a cup full of foaming wine mixed with spices; He pours it out, and all the wicked of the earth drink it down to the very dregs."
Isaiah 51:17 (BSB) "Awake, awake! Rise up, O Jerusalem, you who have drunk from the hand of the LORD the cup of His wrath, you who have drained to its dregs the goblet that makes men stagger."
John 18:11 (BSB) "Jesus said to Peter, 'Put your sword away! Shall I not drink the cup the Father has given Me?'"
Acts 12:2 (BSB) "He had James, the brother of John, put to death with the sword."
🔹 SOCRATIC HINT

James was later killed by Herod (Acts 12:2) and John was exiled to the island of Patmos. So did they in fact "drink the cup" — and what does that tell us about how seriously Christ meant what He said?

→ Yes, they actually did share in Christ's sufferings through persecution and death — Christ's word always comes to pass, even when the one who first hears it doesn't fully grasp it.

📖 After Q2 — ~600 words · ~4:37 elapsed
FULL ANSWER (Reference — hidden, does not affect timing)

The "cup" is first and foremost the cup of God's wrath against sin which Christ, as the covenant surety and substitute for His people, would drain completely at Calvary (Psalm 75:8; Isaiah 51:17; John 18:11). No one but Christ could drink that cup in its fullness — He drank it so that His people never have to. But the cup also refers to the suffering and persecution that all of Christ's followers must share in union with Him.

James and John said "We can" without grasping what they were saying. Yet God in His sovereignty ensured they did indeed drink it: James through martyrdom (Acts 12:2) and John through decades of suffering and exile on Patmos. Christ's words are never empty — they accomplish exactly what He intends, even across years and circumstances the speaker did not fully see.

QUESTION 3 · Matthew 20:23
Jesus says the seats of honour "belong to those for whom they have been prepared by My Father." Does this mean Jesus doesn't know, or that He is less than the Father?
🔹 SOCRATIC HINT — Think about roles and nature separately

The 1689 Confession teaches that the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are one God, fully equal in power, knowledge, and glory. But when the Son came to earth as the Mediator, He voluntarily took on a role of obeying the Father's will. Can a person be fully equal in dignity to someone while still acting within that person's authority in a specific role?

→ Yes — just as an ambassador is fully equal in dignity to the king who sends him while still acting within the king's instructions and not issuing royal decrees on his own.

📖 CROSS-REFERENCES
Ephesians 1:4–5 (BSB) "For He chose us in Him before the foundation of the world to be holy and blameless in His presence. In love He predestined us for adoption as His sons through Jesus Christ, according to the good pleasure of His will."
1689 Confession, Ch. 2 & Ch. 8 "In this divine and infinite Being there are three subsistences, the Father, the Word or Son, and Holy Spirit, of one substance, power, and eternity, each having the whole divine essence…" (Ch. 2) — and Ch. 8 establishes Christ's willing mediatorial subordination in the work of redemption.
📖 After Q3 — ~840 words · ~6:28 elapsed
FULL ANSWER (Reference — hidden, does not affect timing)

This verse is not a denial of Christ's deity or a statement of ignorance. Rather, it is an expression of Christ's voluntary mediatorial subordination in the economy of redemption. As the God-Man — fully God and fully Man, acting in His office as Mediator — Jesus speaks of the Father's prerogative as distinct from His own role as Mediator. The seats of nearest glory in the kingdom have been prepared by the Father's sovereign decree from eternity (Ephesians 1:4–5), and Christ in His mediatorial role acts within those determined purposes.

John Gill carefully notes that this does not suggest any ignorance in the eternal Son. Rather, it reflects the Trinitarian covenant of redemption in which the Father decrees, the Son executes, and the Spirit applies. The 1689 Confession (Ch. 2) is clear that all three Persons are fully equal in divine essence; yet in the economy of salvation, the Son willingly takes the role of the obedient Mediator (Ch. 8). Both are true simultaneously and neither cancels the other.

QUESTION 4 · Matthew 20:25–27
What does Christ teach about true greatness in His kingdom, and what needs to happen inside a person before they can actually live this way?
🔹 SOCRATIC HINT — Contrast the two models

Jesus describes two models of greatness in vv. 25–27: the Gentile rulers "lord it over" people, and Christ's alternative is to become a servant, even a slave. Be honest — which of those two comes naturally to a fallen human heart, and which requires something to change first?

→ Lording it over others comes naturally to a fallen heart — genuine servanthood requires the heart to be changed from the inside by something beyond the person's own power.

🔹 SOCRATIC HINT

Jesus doesn't just command servanthood — He says "just as the Son of Man… came to serve." Why does it matter that the model for this is not a great human example, but the eternal Son of God Himself?

→ Because it means Christlike servanthood is not a human achievement to copy but a divine pattern that can only be reproduced in us by the Spirit of the same Christ who lived it.

📖 CROSS-REFERENCE
Philippians 2:5–7 (BSB) "Let your attitude be the same as that of Christ Jesus: Who, being in very nature God, did not consider equality with God something to be grasped, but made Himself nothing, taking the very nature of a servant, being made in human likeness."
📖 After Q4 — ~1,090 words · ~8:23 elapsed
FULL ANSWER (Reference — hidden, does not affect timing)

True greatness in Christ's kingdom is defined as servanthood — the deliberate choosing of the lowest place for the good of others. The ruler of the Gentiles defines greatness as power over others; Christ defines it as placing oneself under others for their benefit. This is the great reversal that runs through the whole of Matthew 20 and the whole of the Gospel: the last shall be first, the servant shall be great.

Benjamin Keach rightly emphasised that this spirit of servanthood is not natural to fallen human beings. We are by nature curved inward, self-serving, and status-seeking. The capacity to genuinely prefer another's good over our own advancement is a fruit of the Holy Spirit — a direct result of the same sovereign grace that regenerates the heart. And the supreme model is Christ Himself (Philippians 2:5–7), the eternal Son who became a slave for our redemption. We are not merely to admire this example at a distance; the Spirit conforms us to it from the inside out, making us like the Servant-King we worship.

QUESTION 5 · Matthew 20:28
What is the "ransom for many" — and who exactly are "the many" that Christ died for?
🔹 SOCRATIC HINT — Study the Greek word

The Greek behind "ransom" is λύτρον ἀντί — "a price paid instead of / in the place of." If Christ paid a price in someone's place, what does that mean about the people He paid it for — are they merely given a chance, or actually set free?

→ They are actually and certainly set free — a ransom paid in someone's place actually secures their release, it doesn't merely make release possible.

🔹 SOCRATIC HINT — Check Isaiah 53

Read Isaiah 53:11–12 below. The Servant "bears the iniquities" of "the many" and makes them righteous. Does this sound like God merely offering a chance of forgiveness to everyone, or like an actual, effective removal of sin from a specific people?

→ It is an actual, effective removal of sin from a specific people — the Servant truly bears and takes away their iniquities, He does not merely make bearing them possible.

📖 CROSS-REFERENCES
Isaiah 53:11–12 (BSB) "After the anguish of His soul, He will see the light of life and be satisfied; by His knowledge My righteous Servant will justify many, and He will bear their iniquities. Therefore I will give Him the many as His portion, and He will divide the spoils with the strong, because He poured out His life unto death, and was numbered with the transgressors. For He bore the sin of many and made intercession for the transgressors."
1689 Confession, Ch. 8 §5 "The Lord Jesus, by His perfect obedience and sacrifice of Himself… hath fully satisfied the justice of God, procured reconciliation, and purchased an everlasting inheritance in the kingdom of heaven, for all those whom the Father hath given unto Him."
📖 After Q5 — ~1,280 words · ~9:50 elapsed
FULL ANSWER (Reference — hidden, does not affect timing)

The Greek λύτρον ἀντί means a "ransom in the place of" — the language of actual substitution: one person's life standing in for another's. Christ gives His life as the actual payment that releases His people from the debt of sin and the penalty of death.

"For many" — John Gill argues carefully that "many" here (echoing Isaiah 53:11–12) does not mean "all without exception." Rather, it means many as distinguished from one — Christ alone versus the entire company of the elect whom He represents and redeems. The word "many" is used in contrast to Christ as the single substitute, not in contrast to "all." The elect are many; the substitute is one. This is what the 1689 Confession (Ch. 8, §5) calls "the purchase of an everlasting inheritance for all those whom the Father hath given unto Him." Christ did not make salvation merely possible for an undefined multitude; He actually and certainly secured it for His people.

Redemptive-Historical Note: Jesus is consciously reading His own death through Isaiah 53. He is the Suffering Servant Israel had been waiting for, and the word "many" is deliberately borrowed from Isaiah's own language. This is Christ interpreting His own mission through Scripture's own terms.
