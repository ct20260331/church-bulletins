John Gill's Exposition of the New Testament

Matthew 22:23–33 — The Resurrection Defended on Covenantal Grounds

Matthew 22:23

The same day came to him the Sadducees, following the failure of the Pharisees and Herodians. This text confirms that the Sadducees operated as a distinct theological party within Jewish life, completely separate from other factions. Recognizing that prior traps had failed, they approached Christ with a complex, knotty dilemma that they routinely used to puzzle their theological rivals. Their primary goal was to make the orthodox doctrine of the future physical resurrection appear utterly absurd. This materialist school rejected the existence of angels, spiritual realms, and the immortality of the human soul. Historically, this system traces back to Sadoc and Baithus, two students who completely twisted the ethical teachings of Antigonus of Socho concerning future rewards.

  1. Identifies Sadducees as a distinct sect separate from Pharisees and Herodians.
  2. Notes they used a knotty question to nonplus Christ.
  3. Details their denial of the resurrection, angels, and soul's immortality.
  4. Attributes their theological origin to the disciples of Antigonus of Socho.
  5. Traces their specific name directly to the disciple named Sadoc.

Matthew 22:24 Saying, Master, Moses said, referring directly to the core statutory text of Deuteronomy 25:5. The Sadducees do not quote the law verbatim here, but instead summarize its core legislative intent. The ordinance required a surviving brother to marry the widow of a childless man to raise up heirs to his name. This specific familial practice actually pre-dated the formal Sinai covenant, as observed in the older historical narratives concerning Er and Onan. The legislative design aimed to preserve lineage boundaries and keep tribal property inheritances entirely distinct. The law applied only in cases of absolute childlessness; if heirs existed, such a marriage was strictly forbidden by separate judicial statutes.

  1. Cites the levirate law of Deuteronomy 25:5.
  2. Notes the custom pre-dated Moses, seen in Er and Onan.
  3. Identifies its purpose as preserving family lineages and inheritances.
  4. Mentions rabbinic regulations compiled in the tractate Yebamot.

Matthew 22:25 Now there were with us seven brethren, &c. This account could have been an actual historical occurrence, but it is far more likely a highly elaborate hypothetical scenario invented purely to test the limits of resurrection doctrine. The first brother entered a legal marriage, lived for a season with his spouse, and subsequently died without producing any physical issue. Consequently, because there was no heir, he left his widow to his immediate brother in strict accordance with the judicial duties laid out by the Mosaic legal code.

  1. Considers if the case was real or hypothetical.
  2. Notes the first brother married and died childless.
  3. Mentions the legal transfer to the second brother.

Matthew 22:26 The same also the second, and the third, unto the seventh. The narrative describes a mechanical, sequential repetition of the exact same event. The second brother married the widow and died without any heirs; the third brother then wedded her and likewise died childless. This precise cycle continued without exception through the entire family line until all seven brothers had been married to her, and each had passed away leaving zero offspring behind.

  1. Tracks sequential marriages through seven brothers.
  2. Notes all died childless in turn.
  3. Emphasizes complete lack of offspring.

Matthew 22:27 And last of all the woman died also. She survived all seven of her husbands and ultimately passed away without bearing a single child from any of her legal unions. This precise condition of absolute childlessness is deliberately emphasized in the text to shut down any simple legal resolution. It was crafted to prevent any potential heir from claiming preeminence or defining her marital status at the future awakening.

  1. Notes the woman died last of all.
  2. Highlights her total childlessness throughout.
  3. Observes this window complicates resurrection claims.

Matthew 22:28 Therefore in the resurrection, &c. They point forward to the specific future period when the dead shall be raised up into an everlasting state. They demand to know whose wife she will be among the seven men, given the fact that all seven were legally wedded to her on earth. The Sadducees are arguing entirely from the nature of carnal, earthly marriage relations. Because it is a divine institution here, they falsely assume it must continue identically in the world to come, creating an impossible conflict of property and marital rights.

  1. Focuses on conditions of the future state.
  2. Asks whose wife she will be.
  3. Exposes their false assumption about eternal marriage.

Matthew 22:29 Jesus answered and said unto them, ye do err, &c. Our Lord delivered a direct, incisive response designed to fully expose their deep ignorance and silence their public challenge. He explicitly identifies two structural sources for their profound theological errors. First, they lacked a true, spiritual understanding of the Old Testament Scriptures, which repeatedly show that the soul persists after physical death and that bodies will be restored. Second, they completely ignored the absolute, omnipotent power of God, which is fully capable of collecting scattered elements and raising up human bodies from dust.

  1. Explains Christ exposed their ignorance to silence them.
  2. Identifies failure to understand Scripture as a source of error.
  3. Identifies rejection of God's omnipotent power as another source.
  4. Asserts divine power easily raises scattered bodies.

Matthew 22:30 For in the resurrection, &c. In that future state when the soul and body are reunited, carnal relations cease entirely; men no longer take wives, and women are no longer given away in marriage. Instead, the redeemed exist as the angels of God in heaven, possessing a state of perfect immortality and freedom from decay. This comparison does not mean humans become bodiless spirits; rather, it means they are free from physical death. Furthermore, by explicitly mentioning holy angels, Christ delivers a direct presuppositional strike against the wider Sadducean denial of spiritual beings.

  1. States human marriage ceases completely in the resurrection.
  2. Compares saints to angels specifically regarding immortality.
  3. Confirms humans retain physical bodies, unlike angels.
  4. Notes Christ's mention of angels refutes Sadducean materialist theology.

Matthew 22:31 But as touching the resurrection of the dead, &c. To ensure they do not misinterpret His argument as a defense of mere spiritual immortality, Christ shifts focus directly to the literal, bodily resurrection of the dead. He demands, "have ye not read that which was spoken unto you by God?" This specific phrasing establishes that the Old Testament is the direct, uncompromised breath of God, and that its historic declarations remain actively addressed to the living ears of every subsequent generation.

  1. Focuses on literal body resurrection.
  2. Appeals to law spoken by God.
  3. Affirms Scripture addresses contemporary readers.

Matthew 22:32 I am the God of Abraham, and the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob, &c. God is not the God of the dead, but of the living. Though these ancient patriarchs had been physically dead for centuries when God spoke to Moses at the burning bush, the Lord explicitly deployed the grammatical present tense, "I am," rather than the past tense. This proves their souls live continuously in His presence, and because the covenant encompasses the whole person, their physical bodies are guaranteed an ultimate resurrection. The righteous dead are still accounted living by virtue of their unbending union with God.

  1. Analyzes the grammatical present tense 'I am'.
  2. Deduces patriarchs live continuously with God.
  3. Proves covenant relations guarantee physical resurrection.
  4. Validates that righteous dead are called living.

Matthew 22:33 And when the multitude heard this, they were astonished at his teaching. The gathered crowds were utterly amazed by the sheer depth, authority, and structural clarity of His theological argument. He successfully bypassed their artificial grid, completely broke the intellectual power of the Sadducean objection, and clearly vindicated the reality of the physical resurrection using the books of the law itself.

  1. Notes crowd's profound astonishment.
  2. Mentions Christ's clear authority.
  3. Highlights the dismantled objection.
