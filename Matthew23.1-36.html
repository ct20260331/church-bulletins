An Exposition of Matthew 23 — Through a 1689 Federalist & Amillennial Lens

Matthew 23:1

Then spake Jesus to the multitude, and to his disciples. IOur Lord having silenced his adversaries, the Pharisees, Sadducees, and Herodians, and having completely nonplussed them, by an inquiry concerning the Messiah, whose son he was, turns his discourse to the common people, the multitude that surrounded him, and to his own immediate followers, his disciples. IIHe took this opportunity, whilst the Pharisees were together, and before they slunk away, to expose them before the whole body of the people; and that because the time of his death was drawing near, and he should not have many more opportunities: and this he did, not out of any private resentment, but for the glory of God, and the good of the souls of men, IIIthat they might not be deceived by these plausible, but wicked men; and that his disciples might know how to behave towards them, and what to avoid; and that they might be prepared to meet with opposition from them, and not be offended at it.

  1. Christ, having silenced the institutional sects, intentionally redirects his teaching to the common crowd and his close disciples.
  2. He seizes this final pre-crucifixion opportunity to expose these leaders publicly for God's glory, not out of personal malice.
  3. This exposure serves to protect the people from deception and prepare the disciples for upcoming persecution.
Matthew 23:2

Saying, The Scribes and the Pharisees sit in Moses' seat. IBy "Moses' seat" is meant, not a material chair or seat, in which Moses sat, though the Jews talk of a chair of Moses, which they say he made, and after him Joshua, and after him the elders, &c. but the office, authority, and power of teaching the people, and administering the laws of Moses, which he did: and so to sit in his seat, is to succeed him in that office and authority of teaching the people, and interpreting the law. IIThe Scribes and Pharisees did this, they were the common teachers, and ordinary glossators upon the law; they took upon them the reading and expounding of it to the people, and were lookers-after the execution of it: IIIthey were not the chief magistrates, nor judges, though some of them were in the sanhedrim, but they were the standing docents or doctors of the church.

  1. "Moses' seat" refers not to a physical relic, but to the authoritative office of teaching and interpreting the divine law.
  2. The Scribes and Pharisees had fully assumed this role as the regular public expositors and syntax lookers-after.
  3. Their authority here was ecclesiastical rather than civil, acting strictly as the standing doctors of the church.
Matthew 23:3

All therefore whatsoever they bid you observe, that observe and do; Iwhich must be understood, not in an unlimited sense, of everything they should say; for they taught many things which were contrary to the law of God, and made it of none effect by their traditions; but whatsoever they bid you observe and do, according to the law of Moses, which they read and expounded: IIwhilst they sat in Moses' chair, and taught what Moses said, they were to be heard and obeyed; but when they taught for doctrines the commandments of men, they were not to be heeded: but do not ye after their works: for they say, and do not. IIIThey did not live according to the doctrine they taught; their actions did not agree with their words; they preached up justice, mercy, and faith, but were themselves unjust, unmerciful, and faithless.

  1. This command of obedience applies exclusively to their scriptural expositions of Moses, absolutely excluding extra-biblical traditions.
  2. They deserve a hearing only while echoing Moses, but must be completely ignored when preaching human innovations.
  3. Their personal lives are indicted because their practice utterly contradicts their preaching, proving them void of justice and faith.
Matthew 23:4

For they bind heavy burdens and grievous to be borne, and lay them on men's shoulders; IMeaning not the rites and ceremonies of the law of Moses, circumcision, and other rituals, which obliged to the keeping of the whole law, which was a yoke men were not able to bear; but the traditions of the elders, which the Scribes and Pharisees were very tenacious of, and very severely enjoined the observance of. IINow the binding of these heavy things, means the imposing them on men, obliging them to observe them very strictly, under great penalties, should they omit them. IIIbut they themselves will not move them with one of their fingers: the sense is, not that they would not ease them in the least degree, or dispense with their performance of them in the least measure, upon any consideration, though this also was true; but that they made things light for themselves, and made them heavy for others.

  1. These "heavy burdens" refer to the extra-biblical traditions of the elders, not the formal ordinances of the Mosaic law.
  2. The "binding" of these traditions represents the severe enforcement of arbitrary rules upon the conscience under heavy penalties.
  3. They hypocritically exempt themselves from these strict demands while forcing the common people to carry them.
Matthew 23:5

But all their works they do for to be seen of men: IAll their prayers, alms deeds, and fastings, were all done in a public manner, that men might behold them, and they might have applause and glory from them: they sought neither the glory of God, nor the good of their fellow creatures. IIthey make broad their phylacteries: these were four sections of the law, wrote on parchments, folded up in the skin of a clean beast, and tied to the head and hand. These, they imagined, were commanded them by God; and the Pharisees, because they would be thought to be more holy and religious, wore these things broader than the rest of the people; IIIand enlarge the borders of their garments. These were the fringes which they put upon the borders of their garments, and on them a ribbon of blue, to put them in mind of the commandments. They ascribed the like virtue to these fringes, as to their phylacteries, and enlarged them beyond the ordinary size.

  1. Their core motivation is exposed as pure theater, designed solely to win human applause rather than seek the glory of God.
  2. They distorted their phylacteries physically, making the parchment text boxes intentionally larger to fake an elite spirituality.
  3. They unnaturally lengthened their ceremonial garment fringes, treating physical threads as superstitious tokens of superior holiness.
Matthew 23:6

And love the uppermost rooms at feasts, IOr the first and chief places to sit, or lie down on, at ordinary meals, and especially at large entertainments, where the great ones sat, which the Scribes and Pharisees affected. With the Romans, the most honourable place was at the upper end of the table to senior men, and who excelled in prudence and authority. IIand the chief seats in the synagogues; for these were different; the seats of the senior men were turned towards the people, and the backs of them were towards the ark or chest, in which the holy books were put; IIIand these seem to be what the Scribes and Pharisees coveted, that they might be in the full view of the people, sitting first with their faces towards the congregation.

  1. Their intense lust for social status is evident in how they explicitly covet the premier places at banquets.
  2. The "chief seats" were localized elevated benches traditionally reserved for the most senior and prudent men.
  3. They craved these specific synagogue seats because it placed them in full, uninterrupted view of the congregation.
Matthew 23:7

And greetings in the markets, IThey loved to be clapped and complimented in public places of resort, where there was a crowd of people to look at them, and take notice of them; and to have bowed unto them, and their hand kissed, in token of superior respect, and as an acknowledgement of their wisdom and authority. IIand to be called of men, Rabbi, Rabbi; a title of great honour, and which they were very fond of: it was twice repeated, to express their great dignity, and the intense respect men had for them, and to show how much they were delighted with it. IIIIt was a title that began to be given to their doctors about the time of Christ; it was not used by the ancient prophets, nor by the men of the great synagogue, but by the modern ones.

  1. Their love for public salutations and physical bows in crowded markets demonstrates an insatiable desire for societal validation.
  2. Their obsession with the duplicated title "Rabbi, Rabbi" was demanded to inflate their perceived spiritual authority.
  3. This elitist honorific was a modern innovation of contemporary doctors, entirely unknown to ancient prophets.

IIfor one is your Master, even Christ; he is the only Lord, King, and Lawgiver in his church; he is the only prophet, and teacher, whose doctrine is to be received, and whose laws are to be obeyed. IIIand all ye are brethren; you are all upon an equal footing; you have no dominion over one another's faith, nor any authority to bind and loose the consciences of your fellow Christians.

IIThey were not to pin their faith upon any man's sleeve, or give up their judgments to the dictates of any human teacher, however eminent for wisdom and holiness. IIIfor one is your Father, which is in heaven. He is the author of your being, the father of your spirits, and the source of all your spiritual blessings; he is the founder of your faith, and the only disposer of your consciences.

  2. Believers are forbidden from pinning their faith to the sleeve of any human teacher or yielding up their judgment blindly.
  3. God alone is the true Father of spirits, the source of every blessing, and sole ruler of conscience.

But he that is greatest among you shall be your servant. IILet him rather behave himself with all humility and lowliness of mind, and look upon himself as a servant to his fellow Christians; IIIready to do them all the good he can, and to serve them in love, according to the example of Christ, who came not to be ministered unto, but to minister.

Matthew 23:12

And whosoever shall exalt himself shall be abased; IHe that proud and haughty, who seeks his own glory, and affects titles of honour among men; who lifts up himself above his fellow creatures, and is arrogant in his behavior towards God; such an one shall be brought low, either by the broad judgements of God in this world, or at the day of judgement. IIand he that shall humble himself shall be exalted. He that is low in his own eyes, who is sensible of his own unworthiness and weakness, and who is content to be in a low station, and to serve others; IIIsuch an one shall be exalted by God, both in this world, and in that which is to come; he shall have more grace given him here, and glory hereafter.

IThis is another instance of their corrupt and ridiculous doctrine concerning oaths.   2. Their rules dictated that swearing by the dedicated gift or corban laid upon that altar created an unalterable, binding covenant.

Matthew 23:19

Ye fools and blind: for whether is greater, the gift, or the altar that sanctifieth the gift? IIIThe gift or sacrifice, before it is brought to the altar, is common, but when it is laid upon it, it becomes holy; and therefore, according to all rules of reason, that which sanctifies must be greater than that which is sanctified by it; so that they were entirely wrong in preferring the gift to the altar.

  1. Christ repeats his sharp rebuke of their systemic blindness to reassert that the altar logically transcends the gift.

Ye serpents, ye generation of vipers, Iour Lord here throws off all reserve, and calls them by their proper names, which accurately described their nature and character. They were serpents for their subtlety, craft, and malice; and a generation of vipers, as being the poisonous offspring of malignant ancestors. IIhow can ye escape the damnation of hell? This is not an inquiry of curiosity, but a strong negation, implying that it was impossible they should escape it, remaining as they were. IIITheir hypocrisy, extortion, and murder of the prophets, wrapped up in final impenitence, made their damnation sure and inevitable; no ritual washings or high titles could screen them from the wrath of God.

  1. Christ threw off all reserve to label them serpents for their deep malice, and vipers for their poisonous heritage.
  2. His question acts as a definitive logical negation, establishing the total impossibility of escaping eternal wrath apart from repentance.
  3. Hoarded hypocrisy, extortion, and final impenitence locked them into an absolute, inescapable sentence of divine condemnation.
Matthew 23:34

Wherefore, behold, I send unto you prophets, and wise men, and scribes: Imeaning his apostles, evangelists, and ministers of the New Testament; who should be endued with the Holy Ghost, and gifts suitable for their work. He speaks as the Lord and Sovereign of the church, who has the sole power of sending ministers; IIand he calls them by these ancient names, to show that they should be equal to the old prophets in inspiration, and should excel their own wise men and scribes in true wisdom and learning. IIIand some of them ye shall kill and crucify; and some of them shall ye scourge in your synagogues, and persecute them from city to city: which was literally fulfilled in the treatment they gave to Stephen, James, Peter, John, Paul, and others, showing that their enmity against Christ was unquenchable.

  1. This text demonstrates Christ's divine sovereignty, where he independently authorizes and sends New Testament ministers.
  2. Ancient titles are applied to prove his messengers would thoroughly outshine the Pharisees' scholars in genuine theological wisdom.
  3. Literal fulfillment of these predicted martyrdoms and synagogue scourgings occurred in the historical book of Acts.
Matthew 23:35

That upon you may come all the righteous blood shed upon the earth, Inot that they should be punished for sins they did not commit, but that by walking in the same steps, and filling up the measure of iniquity, they should bring upon themselves the punishment due to all the righteous blood shed from the beginning. IIfrom the blood of righteous Abel unto the blood of Zacharias son of Barachias, whom ye slew between the temple and the altar. This Zacharias was either the prophet Zechariah, or rather Jehoiada's son, who was stoned in the court of the house of the Lord. IIIChrist mentions these two as the first and last instances of righteous blood recorded in the Old Testament, to show that God remembers all the injuries done to his servants, and will avenge them on that wicked generation.

  1. They were not punished for crimes they did not commit, but bore the cumulative weight of historical guilt.
  2. Zacharias is contextually identified as the faithful martyr stoned within the holy courts of the Lord's house.
  3. These two names frame the complete historical scope of Old Testament martyrdom, proving God tracks and avenges his servants.
Matthew 23:36

Verily I say unto you, All these things shall come upon this generation. IOur Lord wraps up this part of his discourse with a solemn asseveration, or oath, confirming the certainty of the destruction he had predicted. II"All these things," meaning the judgments of God, famine, pestilence, sword, and captivity, and the utter desolation of their land, shall come upon the very people that were then living. IIIThis was fully accomplished within forty years, when Jerusalem was destroyed by the Romans; and it stands as an awful warning of the danger of rejecting the Messiah, and persecuting his faithful ministers, showing that God's patience has its limits, and his judgments are sure.

  1. Christ closes this section with a solemn oath to guarantee the absolute certainty of impending judicial destruction.
  2. "All these things" refers to specific historical judgments of sword, famine, and captivity falling upon the contemporary population.
  3. Historical validation of this prophecy occurred within forty years via the Roman army, marking the outer boundary of divine patience.
