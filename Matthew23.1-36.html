Matthew 23:1

IOur Lord having silenced his adversaries, the Pharisees, Sadducees, and Herodians, and having completely nonplussed them, by an inquiry concerning the Messiah, whose son he was, turns his discourse to the common people, the multitude that surrounded him, and to his own immediate followers, his disciples. IIHe took this opportunity, whilst the Pharisees were together, and before they slunk away, to expose them before the whole body of the people; and that because the time of his death was drawing near, and he should not have many more opportunities: and this he did, not out of any private resentment, but for the glory of God, and the good of the souls of men, IIIthat they might not be deceived by these plausible, but wicked men; and that his disciples might know how to behave towards them, and what to avoid; and that they might be prepared to meet with opposition from them, and not be offended at it.

  1. I note that Christ, having silenced the institutional sects, intentionally redirects his teaching to the common crowd and his close disciples.
  2. I observe that he seizes this final pre-crucifixion opportunity to expose these leaders publicly for God's glory, not out of personal malice.
  3. I emphasize that this exposure serves to protect the people from deception and prepare the disciples for upcoming persecution.
Matthew 23:2

IIThe Scribes and Pharisees did this, they were the common teachers, and ordinary glossators upon the law; they took upon them the reading and expounding of it to the people, and were lookers-after the execution of it: IIIthey were not the chief magistrates, nor judges, though some of them were in the sanhedrim, but they were the standing docents or doctors of the church.

  1. I define "Moses' seat" not as a physical relic, but as the authoritative office of teaching and interpreting the divine law.
  2. I point out that the Scribes and Pharisees had fully assumed this role as the regular public expositors and syntax lookers-after.
  3. I clarify that their authority here was ecclesiastical rather than civil, acting strictly as the standing doctors of the church.

IThis is an ironical concession, or a prophecy delivered in an imperative form; signaling that they were left to themselves to commit that last and supreme wickedness, which would fill up the cup of their iniquity, and bring down swift destruction upon them. IIThey were to go on as their fathers had done, and finish what they had begun; IIItheir fathers killed the prophets, and they were to kill the Messiah, and then the measure would be full, and wrath would come upon them to the uttermost, to the total ruin of their nation, city, and temple.

  1. I interpret this command ironically as a prophecy of judicial abandonment, where they are left to consummate their supreme wickedness.
  2. I see this as a divine decree allowing them to complete the baseline trajectory of sin that their ancestors first established.
  3. I declare that by crucifying the Messiah, they will completely fill their cup of guilt, triggering the total ruin of their nation.
Matthew 23:33

Ye serpents, ye generation of vipers, Iour Lord here throws off all reserve, and calls them by their proper names, which accurately described their nature and character. They were serpents for their subtlety, craft, and malice; and a generation of vipers, as being the poisonous offspring of malignant ancestors. IIhow can ye escape the damnation of hell? This is not an inquiry of curiosity, but a strong negation, implying that it was impossible they should escape it, remaining as they were. IIITheir hypocrisy, extortion, and murder of the prophets, wrapped up in final impenitence, made their damnation sure and inevitable; no ritual washings or high titles could screen them from the wrath of God.

  1. I highlight Christ throwing off all reserve to label them serpents for their deep malice, and vipers for their poisonous heritage.
  2. I explain his question as a definitive logical negation, establishing the total impossibility of their escaping eternal wrath apart from repentance.
  3. I conclude that their hoarded hypocrisy, extortion, and final impenitence lock them into an absolute, inescapable sentence of divine condemnation.
Matthew 23:34

Wherefore, behold, I send unto you prophets, and wise men, and scribes: Imeaning his apostles, evangelists, and ministers of the New Testament; who should be endued with the Holy Ghost, and gifts suitable for their work. He speaks as the Lord and Sovereign of the church, who has the sole power of sending ministers; IIand he calls them by these ancient names, to show that they should be equal to the old prophets in inspiration, and should excel their own wise men and scribes in true wisdom and learning. IIIand some of them ye shall kill and crucify; and some of them shall ye scourge in your synagogues, and persecute them from city to city: which was literally fulfilled in the treatment they gave to Stephen, James, Peter, John, Paul, and others, showing that their enmity against Christ was unquenchable.

  1. I view this text as a clear demonstration of Christ's divine sovereignty, where he independently authorizes and sends his New Testament ministers.
  2. I note that he applies these ancient names to prove his messengers would thoroughly outshine the Pharisees' scholars in genuine theological wisdom.
  3. I trace the literal fulfillment of these predicted martyrdoms and synagogue scourgings in the historical book of Acts through the persecution of the apostles.
Matthew 23:35

That upon you may come all the righteous blood shed upon the earth, Inot that they should be punished for sins they did not commit, but that by walking in the same steps, and filling up the measure of iniquity, they should bring upon themselves the punishment due to all the righteous blood shed from the beginning. IIfrom the blood of righteous Abel unto the blood of Zacharias son of Barachias, whom ye slew between the temple and the altar. This Zacharias was either the prophet Zechariah, or rather Jehoiada's son, who was stoned in the court of the house of the Lord. IIIChrist mentions these two as the first and last instances of righteous blood recorded in the Old Testament, to show that God remembers all the injuries done to his servants, and will avenge them on that wicked generation.

  1. I clarify that they are not being punished for crimes they did not commit, but are bearing the cumulative weight of historical guilt.
  2. I evaluate the identity of Zacharias, identifying him contextually as the faithful martyr stoned within the holy courts of the Lord's house.
  3. I see these two names as framing the complete historical scope of Old Testament martyrdom, proving God accurately tracks and avenges his servants.
Matthew 23:36

Verily I say unto you, All these things shall come upon this generation. IOur Lord wraps up this part of his discourse with a solemn asseveration, or oath, confirming the certainty of the destruction he had predicted. II"All these things," meaning the judgments of God, famine, pestilence, sword, and captivity, and the utter desolation of their land, shall come upon the very people that were then living. IIIThis was fully accomplished within forty years, when Jerusalem was destroyed by the Romans; and it stands as an awful warning of the danger of rejecting the Messiah, and persecuting his faithful ministers, showing that God's patience has its limits, and his judgments are sure.

  1. I observe that Christ closes this section with a solemn oath to guarantee the absolute certainty of the impending judicial destruction.
  2. I define "all these things" as the specific historical judgments of sword, famine, and captivity that fell directly upon the contemporary population.
  3. I witness the historical validation of this prophecy within forty years via the Roman army, marking the outer boundary of divine patience.
